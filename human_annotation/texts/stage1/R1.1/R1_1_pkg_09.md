```
We formalize V2G fleet aggregation as a sequential decision problem
under dual constraints. An aggregator manages N electric vehicles
across T discrete 30-minute steps, minimizing operating cost subject
to vehicle quality-of-service and market commitment requirements.

The aggregator operates in two levels: an aggregate planner emits a
fleet-level power signal p(t), positive for charging and negative
for V2G discharge, decomposed as p(t) = c(t) - d(t) with bounded
charging and discharging powers. A vehicle-level dispatcher then
realizes the aggregate target through per-vehicle commands.
Strategies differ only in how p(t) is computed.

Energy settlement is asymmetric, with the buy-sell spread common
to all three markets in our evaluation. The optimization objective
minimizes a weighted sum of energy cost, quality-of-service penalty,
and commitment penalty, with weights chosen either fixed a priori
or learned during training.

Our central contribution is a unified evaluation framework for
comparing constrained reinforcement learning with optimization-based
schedulers on the V2G commitment problem. The framework introduces
the deployment-gap metric and the commitment reliability frontier,
enabling direct comparison across heterogeneous strategies. We use
this framework to position constrained RL relative to model-based
coupling.

Individual vehicle tracking scales as O(NT), so we adopt an
aggregate distribution representation with K SOC bins and M
remaining-time bins, reducing planning complexity. Aggregate
dynamics follow standard transition equations with arrivals and
departures.
```

```
## Main issues

**1. The contribution claim is ambiguous and risks overstatement.**
The phrase "unified evaluation framework" is doing heavy lifting. Two things are bundled: (a) a benchmarking setup that compares constrained RL against optimization-based schedulers, and (b) two specific instruments — the "deployment-gap metric" and the "commitment reliability frontier." Whether either instrument is genuinely new is not stated. "Unified" and "enabling direct comparison across heterogeneous strategies" are strong claims; consider softening to "comparable evaluation" unless the heterogeneity is precisely characterized (which strategy classes, what was previously incomparable, and why).

Also, "position constrained RL relative to model-based coupling" is vague — does the work show RL is competitive, dominated, complementary, or merely characterizable? A neutral phrasing such as "characterize the trade-offs between …" avoids implying a favorable result before evidence is presented.

**2. Logical ordering within the section is uneven.**
The flow goes: problem → two-level architecture → cost model → contribution → state representation. The contribution paragraph interrupts the formulation.
```
