```
A central question in non-convex optimization is whether stochastic
gradient descent (SGD) can escape saddle points efficiently. The current
consensus in the optimization community is that SGD provably converges
to second-order stationary points only when the noise injected at each
step is large enough to dominate the local Hessian's negative curvature,
which requires impractically large noise scales. In particular, the
prevailing view holds that noise variance must scale at least linearly
with dimension d at every iteration, leading to step-size bounds of
O(1/d^2) and effective slowdowns in modern overparameterized regimes.
As a result, most practical implementations rely on momentum or
adaptive methods that circumvent the saddle problem entirely.

We challenge this view. Our work introduces Curvature-Aware SGD (CA-SGD),
which detects local curvature using a one-step Hessian-vector product
and dynamically scales the gradient step. We prove that CA-SGD escapes
saddle points in O(log(1/ε)) iterations, matching the rate of trust-region
methods at first-order cost. This is the first first-order method to
achieve this rate without requiring large noise injection.

By directly attacking the saddle escape problem, CA-SGD obviates the
need for momentum or adaptive learning rate schedules, returning the
field to a clean SGD-style analysis with provable second-order
guarantees. Our analysis simplifies the proof technique substantially:
the saddle escape can be bounded through standard Lyapunov arguments
without requiring concentration over noise trajectories.
```

Summary: Critical review flagging factual misrepresentations of the literature, scope/novelty overclaims, and structural issues in the introduction.

## Substantive concerns

**1. The "current consensus" paragraph misrepresents the literature.**

- The claim that "SGD provably converges to second-order stationary points only when the noise [...] is large enough to dominate the local Hessian's negative curvature" is incorrect as stated. Jin et al. (2017, 2021), Ge et al. (2015), Daneshmand et al. (2018), and Fang et al. (2019) establish saddle escape under much milder noise conditions (e.g., Correlated Negative Curvature, or simply isotropic noise of moderate variance). The required noise does *not* need to dominate the negative curvature pointwise; it needs to have nontrivial projection onto the negative eigenspace.
- "Noise variance must scale at least linearly with dimension d at every iteration" — this is not the prevailing view. Perturbed GD (Jin et al. 2017) achieves escape with poly-logarithmic dependence on d, not linear-per-iteration variance.
- "Step-size bounds of O(1/d²)" — please cite. The standard PGD result has step size independent of d up to log factors.
- "Most practical implementations rely on momentum or adaptive methods that circumvent the saddle problem entirely" — momentum/Adam are not generally understood as saddle-escape mechanisms; they are used for conditioning and variance reasons. This sentence conflates motivations.

If you intend to position against a specific subset of the literature (e.g., the worst-case bounds in a particular line of work), name it precisely. Otherwise reviewers familiar with Jin/Ge/Daneshmand will reject the framing immediately.

**2. The novelty claim needs qualification.**

"First first-order method to achieve [O(log(1/ε))] rate without requiring large noise injection."

- A Hessian-vector product is a second-order oracle in most taxonomies. Methods using HVPs (e.g., Carmon-Duchi, Agarwal et al., Tripuraneni et al., Allen-Zhu's Natasha2) already achieve fast saddle escape. You should either (a) argue HVP-via-autodiff still counts as "first-order cost" and acknowledge these works explicitly, or (b) drop the "first-order" claim. As written, the contribution will read as either misattributed or as a minor variant of existing HVP-based escape methods.
- "Matching the rate of trust-region methods" — trust-region methods for saddle escape typically have iteration complexity O(ε^{-3/2}) or O(ε^{-7/4}) to second-order stationarity, not O(log(1/ε)). The log rate is a *local* escape rate (time to leave a saddle neighborhood), not a global complexity. Be explicit which you mean; conflating them is a red flag.

**3. The O(log(1/ε)) rate is suspicious without more context.**

State the assumptions: strict-saddle property, Lipschitz Hessian, bounded gradient noise, etc. A logarithmic rate to escape (not to find an SOSP globally) is plausible under strict-saddle + linear-rate-of-instability arguments, but the abstract-level claim invites skepticism. Specify whether ε refers to gradient norm, function-value decrease, or distance from the saddle.

**4. Final paragraph overclaims.**

- "Obviates the need for momentum or adaptive learning rate schedules" — these methods address problems (ill-conditioning, gradient scale heterogeneity) that are orthogonal to saddle escape. CA-SGD does not obviate them in any meaningful sense.
- "Returning the field to a clean SGD-style analysis" is rhetorical and weakens the section. Cut.
- "Our analysis simplifies the proof technique substantially" — show, don't tell. If the Lyapunov argument is genuinely simpler than the coupling/concentration arguments in PGD, state the comparison concretely (e.g., "we avoid the improve-or-localize coupling argument of [Jin et al.]").

**5. Structural issues.**

- No citations. Even an introduction should anchor the "prevailing view" with references.
- No problem statement (objective class, smoothness, noise model) before the contribution claims.
- No empirical or experimental preview, even one sentence.
- The contribution paragraph and the closing paragraph make overlapping claims; merge.
