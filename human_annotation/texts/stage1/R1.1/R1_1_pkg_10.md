We propose a model-based estimator for lithium plating onset in NCM
batteries during fast charging. Our approach combines an electrochemical
reduced-order model (ROM) with a Kalman filter that fuses voltage,
current, and surface temperature measurements. We claim that the
estimator detects plating events 4–7 minutes earlier than
terminal-voltage-based methods.

The ROM tracks lithium concentration in the negative electrode using a
single-particle model with electrolyte dynamics. Plating onset is
identified as the moment the negative electrode surface overpotential
crosses zero. We validate the estimator on a fleet of 14 cells cycled
at 2C, 3C, and 4C rates.

In our experiments, the estimator's predicted plating-onset timestamps
correlate with post-mortem capacity loss measurements at r=0.78. Cells
whose estimator triggered an early plating warning showed an average
12% capacity retention advantage over cells where the warning fired
late, after 200 cycles. Onset timestamps were extracted by thresholding
the estimated overpotential at zero; we did not perform direct
differential-voltage cross-validation due to instrumentation limits
at the cell level.

We argue that this demonstrates the practical value of early plating
detection for fast-charging strategies. The estimator can be deployed
online with sub-millisecond compute cost per timestep on standard
automotive microcontrollers. We also note that the estimator's output
can be combined with cell-balancing controllers to maximize fleet-level
capacity retention, although this integration is beyond the scope of
the present work.
