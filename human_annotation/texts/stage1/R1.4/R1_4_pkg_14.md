```
A central question in non-convex optimization is whether stochastic
gradient descent (SGD) can escape saddle points efficiently. The current
consensus in the optimization community is that SGD provably converges
to second-order stationary points only when the noise injected at each
step is large enough to dominate the local Hessian's negative curvature,
which requires impractically large noise scales. In particular, the
prevailing view holds that noise variance must scale at least linearly
with dimension d at every iteration, leading to step-size bounds of
O(1/d^2) and effective slowdowns in modern overparameterized regimes.
As a result, most practical implementations rely on momentum or
adaptive methods that circumvent the saddle problem entirely.

We challenge this view. Our work introduces Curvature-Aware SGD (CA-SGD),
which detects local curvature using a one-step Hessian-vector product
and dynamically scales the gradient step. We prove that CA-SGD escapes
saddle points in O(log(1/ε)) iterations, matching the rate of trust-region
methods at first-order cost. This is the first first-order method to
achieve this rate without requiring large noise injection.

By directly attacking the saddle escape problem, CA-SGD obviates the
need for momentum or adaptive learning rate schedules, returning the
field to a clean SGD-style analysis with provable second-order
guarantees. Our analysis simplifies the proof technique substantially:
the saddle escape can be bounded through standard Lyapunov arguments
without requiring concentration over noise trajectories.
```

Summary: Reviewed the introduction for framing accuracy, internal logical coherence, novelty positioning, and overclaim risk. The section reads as strongly written but contains several load-bearing claims that are either misrepresented, internally inconsistent, or oversold relative to what a first-order method can plausibly deliver.

**Major issues**

1. **Mischaracterization of the prior literature.** The opening paragraph asserts a "consensus" that SGD escapes saddles only with noise large enough to "dominate the local Hessian's negative curvature." This is not an accurate summary of the standard results (Ge et al. 2015; Jin et al. 2017, 2021; Daneshmand et al. 2018; Fang et al. 2019). The perturbed-SGD line of work shows escape with *isotropic perturbations of modest magnitude*, and the dimension dependence in modern results is polylogarithmic, not linear. The claim that "noise variance must scale at least linearly with dimension d at every iteration, leading to step-size bounds of O(1/d²)" should be cited to a specific theorem; otherwise the contrast you draw is built on a strawman. I would either (a) attribute the claim precisely or (b) soften the framing.

2. **The "first first-order method" claim is likely an overclaim.** Several existing first-order methods escape saddles in $\tilde{O}(\log(1/\varepsilon))$ or near-linear rates without large noise injection — e.g., Neon/Neon2 (Allen-Zhu & Li 2018), which extract negative curvature directions via Hessian-vector products built from gradient differences, and the line of work on accelerated saddle escape. Note also that a method using a one-step Hessian-vector product is on the boundary of what is commonly called "first-order"; HVPs are typically classified as *Hessian-free* second-order information. The claim of being "the first first-order method to achieve this rate" should be tightened, and the HVP-vs-first-order taxonomy needs to be explicit. As written, this is the most exposed sentence in the section.

3. **Internal inconsistency on the role of noise.** Paragraph 1 frames the problem as "noise must be large to escape." Paragraph 2 says CA-SGD escapes "without requiring large noise injection." Paragraph 3 then says the analysis avoids "concentration over noise trajectories." It is unclear whether CA-SGD is a *deterministic* curvature-aware step, a stochastic method with small noise, or stochastic with structured noise. If it is deterministic given the HVP, then the comparison to SGD's noise-driven escape is apples-to-oranges, and the contribution should be reframed as "curvature-aware deterministic correction" rather than as beating SGD at its own game. As stated, a reader will compare it to global complexity bounds like $\tilde{O}(\varepsilon^{-7/4})$ and conclude something far stronger than you mean. State the assumption set and whether $\varepsilon$ refers to gradient norm, the saddle gap, or something else.

5. **"Matching the rate of trust-region methods at first-order cost."** Trust-region methods for second-order stationarity typically have iteration complexity $\tilde{O}(\varepsilon^{-3/2})$ for $\varepsilon$-SOSP, not $O(\log(1/\varepsilon))$. The matched quantity here is presumably the *local* linear escape rate, not the global complexity. This conflation will be flagged by reviewers; please disambiguate.

6. **"Returning the field to a clean SGD-style analysis."** This is rhetorically large.
