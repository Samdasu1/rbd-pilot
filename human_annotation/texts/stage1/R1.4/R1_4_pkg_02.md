## C. Agent output (평가 대상)

```
Summary: I tightened the prose and framing, but the strongest improvement here is removing several overclaims that the original artifact's own evidence does not support. A revised passage and a list of issues follow.

## Key issues in the original

1. **"First analytical tool" is unsupported and likely false.** Reliability curves / CDFs of commitment or service quality are standard in power-systems and stochastic-scheduling literature (chance-constrained dispatch, reserve adequacy curves, LOLP/EUE analyses). Claiming primacy without a literature review is an overclaim and weakens the paper.
2. **Internal contradiction.** The third paragraph says "learned constraints offer no practical advantage over the unconstrained baseline," but the second paragraph reports PPO failing at 0.50 and CMDP surviving until 0.55 — i.e., the learned constraint *does* extend feasibility, just not enough to matter for high-threshold markets. Either soften the claim or restrict it to the high-threshold regime.
3. **Scope of the claim drifts.** The passage shifts from "our frontier on these strategies in this study" to "applicable to any commitment-bound stochastic scheduling problem." Generality has not been demonstrated; this should be posed as a conjecture or future direction, not a result.
4. **Numerical claims need provenance.** Thresholds 0.50, 0.55, 0.95 and "full eligibility across all seeds and markets" should cite the figure/table and state the seed count, market set, and confidence level (or that these are empirical rates over a finite sample, not guarantees).
5. **"100% feasibility through threshold 0.95"** is a finite-sample statement. Without a confidence interval or a statement like "across N=K seeds," it reads as a population guarantee. For market-eligibility framing this matters: a deployment claim built on a handful of seeds should be hedged.
6. **"Cannot serve these markets at all under our deployment protocol"** is fine, but the qualifier "under our deployment protocol" should be repeated when the eligibility conclusion is restated, since changing the protocol (e.g., derating, reserve stacking) could change eligibility.
7. **Redundancy.** "The frontier reveals…" appears three times in four paragraphs; the contribution is restated three times.

## Suggested revision

> Beyond aggregate means, market participation requires that delivered commitment exceed a minimum threshold with high probability. Using the reliability curve defined in Section [X], we plot each strategy's empirical feasibility rate as a function of the commitment threshold τ (Figure [Y]).
>
> The curves expose a feasibility–stringency tradeoff that aggregate statistics obscure. Empirically, PPO's feasibility collapses to zero by τ = 0.50 and CMDP's by τ = 0.55, while the combined strategy retains 100% feasibility across all evaluated seeds up to τ = 0.95. The learned constraint (CMDP) thus yields only a marginal extension over the unconstrained baseline (PPO) and, in the high-threshold regime relevant to ancillary services, no practical advantage.
>
> For services requiring τ ≥ 0.90, only the combined strategy and the predictive LP qualify under our deployment protocol; the combined strategy achieves full eligibility across all evaluated seeds and market settings (N = [k] seeds, [m] market configurations). PPO and CMDP do not qualify for these markets under this protocol.
>
> We view the reliability frontier as a useful diagnostic for matching scheduling strategies to commitment-bound markets. While we have applied it here to V2G dispatch, the construction — plotting the CDF of delivered commitment against a stringency threshold — extends naturally to other commitment-bound stochastic scheduling problems; demonstrating that generality is left to future work.

## Smaller prose notes

- "sharp crossover points that map directly to market eligibility" → "threshold crossings that map onto market-eligibility cutoffs" (less salesy, more precise).
- "tail-risk behavior that determines whether a strategy can serve high-commitment markets" is fine; keep it, but only once.
- Prefer τ
```
