```
Vehicle-to-grid technology turns electric-vehicle fleets into distributed
energy assets that can provide ancillary services to power systems. To
participate in day-ahead energy markets, frequency containment reserves,
or demand response programs, an aggregator must commit to a power
schedule in advance and deliver it reliably during operation; failure
to honor committed schedules triggers financial penalties. Commitment
reliability is therefore not merely desirable but a hard prerequisite
for commercial V2G operation.

Reinforcement learning has emerged as an attractive paradigm for V2G
scheduling because it adapts to stochastic fleet arrivals and volatile
prices without explicit system models. Our work introduces a constrained
RL approach that, to our knowledge, is the first to evaluate commitment
reliability under operational rolling re-planning conditions across
multiple electricity markets. Existing studies treat commitment as a
soft reward penalty; we instead treat it as an explicit constraint
via Lagrangian relaxation.

Our experiments confirm that soft penalties are dominated by arbitrage
incentives — PPO agents achieve only modest commitment under rolling
re-planning, indistinguishable from a commitment-agnostic policy. The
constrained MDP formulation drives training commitment toward 90%
in some markets, though deployment performance differs substantially
from training performance. We position this work as the first study
to quantify the deployment gap in constrained V2G reinforcement
learning.
```

**Main concerns with the original**

1. **The thesis is buried.** The first paragraph motivates V2G commitment in general; the second paragraph mixes method (Lagrangian-constrained RL) with two distinct novelty claims (first multi-market reliability evaluation under rolling re-planning; first to quantify the deployment gap); the third paragraph reports results that actually point to a single, sharper contribution: *constrained RL improves training-time commitment but does not close the deployment gap*. That is a more interesting and more defensible story than "we propose constrained RL for V2G," and it should be the frame from sentence one.

2. **Two "first to" claims, both fragile.** "First to evaluate commitment reliability under operational rolling re-planning conditions across multiple electricity markets" and "first study to quantify the deployment gap in constrained V2G reinforcement learning" are each narrow enough that they may be technically true, but they read as engineered novelty. I'd recommend collapsing to one claim, hedging it ("to our knowledge"), and letting the empirical finding carry the weight. Whether either claim survives a thorough literature check is something I cannot verify.

3. **Internal tension between the second and third paragraphs.** Paragraph 2 sells the constrained formulation as the fix; paragraph 3 admits it largely doesn't work at deployment. As written, this reads as undermining rather than as a deliberate finding. The revision below makes the negative result the point.

4. **Soft, slightly evaluative phrasing.** "Attractive paradigm," "merely desirable," "dominated by arbitrage incentives" — these can be tightened or made more precise. "Drives training commitment toward 90% in some markets" is vague (which markets? what does "toward" mean?); if the number is reportable, report it; if not, say "approaches 90% in favorable market conditions."
