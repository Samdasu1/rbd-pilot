Summary: Critique of the section, focused on substantive issues (overclaiming, anonymity violations, missing baselines, weak evaluation) rather than prose polish.

## Major issues

**1. "First" and "clinically relevant accuracy" are unsupported.**
- "First machine learning method for predicting drug-target binding affinity..." is almost certainly false. There is a large prior literature (e.g., DeepDTA, OnionNet, GNINA, DiffDock-family scoring, and many GNN-based affinity predictors). At minimum this needs to be softened and supported with a comparison table.
- "Clinically relevant accuracy" is a strong, unjustified claim. MAEs of 0.79–0.91 kcal/mol correspond to ~3–5x error in Kd/Ki. You need to either (a) drop this phrase, or (b) define what threshold counts as clinically relevant and cite the source. As written, reviewers will flag this immediately.

**2. Anonymization is broken.**
- "Our previously published dataset (Anonymous Authors et al., 2021)" and "our group's recent crystallography campaigns" both deanonymize. If the venue is double-blind, citing your own prior work as "our" defeats the purpose; standard practice is to cite in third person ("Smith et al., 2021") and let the reader treat it as any other reference. "Our group's crystallography campaigns" is worse — it's an identifying claim that no other group could make. Rewrite to refer to the dataset neutrally and describe the 50K additions without claiming provenance.
- Same issue with "DrugDock-v1 (Anonymous Authors et al., 2022)." The name continuity itself ("DrugDock") plus self-citation likely identifies the group. Consider whether the system name should also be anonymized for submission.

**3. Missing or weak baselines.**
- The 28% improvement is over "a chemistry-only baseline that we constructed for this paper." Self-constructed baselines are the weakest possible comparison. You need published external baselines: at minimum DeepDTA, a docking-based scoring function (Vina/Glide/GNINA), and a recent SOTA GNN affinity predictor. Without these, the headline numbers are not interpretable.
- The DrugDock-v1 comparison is fine as an ablation but does not establish external SOTA.

**4. Evaluation rigor is underspecified.**
- "Held-out test set" — split how? Random, scaffold, target-based, time-based? For binding affinity, random splits dramatically overestimate performance due to ligand and target leakage. State the split protocol and ideally report multiple (e.g., scaffold + target-stratified).
- No variance reporting. MAEs of 0.84 / 0.91 / 0.79 — are these significantly different across classes? Report standard deviation across seeds or bootstrap CIs. The claim of "consistent across classes" needs a statistical basis.
- Three target classes is not "diverse" enough to support "broad applicability." Kinases, GPCRs, and proteases are all well-represented in public databases. The claim weakens or strengthens depending on how training data was distributed across these classes — disclose this.

**5. The 12% / 28% framing.**
- Relative MAE reductions sound large but can be small in absolute terms and not statistically significant. Report absolute MAE for all conditions and significance tests.

**6. Deployment claim is overreach.**
- "Meaningful step toward deployable ML methods in early-stage drug discovery" and "broad applicability in the pharmaceutical industry" are not supported by what's shown. A retrospective MAE on three target classes does not establish deployability. Either provide a prospective validation (held-out targets, blind predictions, experimental follow-up) or remove the deployment framing. As written, this reads as marketing and will draw reviewer skepticism.
