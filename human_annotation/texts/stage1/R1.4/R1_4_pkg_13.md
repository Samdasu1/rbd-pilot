We introduce GraphFlow, a graph neural network architecture that
improves on prior work by allowing message-passing layers to share
parameters across edge types in a typed graph. Our key innovation is
the parameter-sharing scheme, which reduces the model's parameter
count by 30-40% while maintaining accuracy.

We evaluate GraphFlow on three benchmarks: OGB-Products, OGB-Arxiv,
and Reddit. On OGB-Products, GraphFlow achieves 81.7% accuracy
compared to 81.2% for our previous work GraphFlow-v1 (Anonymous
Authors et al., 2022). On OGB-Arxiv, we achieve 73.4% compared to
73.1% for GraphFlow-v1. On Reddit, we achieve 96.1% compared to
95.9% for GraphFlow-v1.

The parameter-sharing mechanism is described in Section 3. The
intuition is that shared parameters act as a regularizer in the
typed-edge setting, which is particularly beneficial for graphs where
some edge types are sparse. Section 4's ablation confirms that
disabling the sharing reduces accuracy by approximately 1.0% on
average.

We position GraphFlow as a successor to the GraphFlow-v1 architecture,
building on its message-passing framework with the additional
parameter-sharing innovation. We expect this work to be a useful step
forward for the practical deployment of GNNs on heterogeneous graphs.
We further note that the parameter-sharing scheme is straightforward to
implement on top of any standard message-passing backbone, requiring
fewer than 100 lines of modification to typical PyTorch Geometric code,
which we believe lowers the bar for adoption.

> We introduce GraphFlow, a message-passing GNN for typed (heterogeneous) graphs in which parameters are shared across edge types rather than instantiated per type. ## Issues you should address before this gets stronger

**1. The headline claim is overstated relative to the evidence.**
The text says the scheme "maintain[s] accuracy," but the numbers reported are *small improvements* (0.2, 0.3, 0.5 points), not parity. Either:
- (a) Lead with parameter efficiency at matched accuracy and treat the small deltas as noise (honest, and what I did above), or
- (b) Defend the gains as real — which requires reporting mean ± std over multiple seeds, and ideally a significance test. Single-number comparisons on OGB are not convincing.

Pick one. The current text tries to have both and undercuts itself.

**2. The novelty framing is thin.**
"Parameter sharing across edge types" is not new in the heterogeneous-GNN literature (e.g., basis/block-decomposition in R-GCN, weight-tying schemes in CompGCN and HGT-style models all live in this space). The draft compares only against the authors' own GraphFlow-v1. Without positioning against this prior art, the contribution looks larger than it is. You need at least a paragraph distinguishing the specific sharing scheme from existing decomposition/tying approaches, and ideally a baseline comparison against one of them.

Reduced relative to *what*? GraphFlow-v1? A per-edge-type parameterization? A specific R-GCN configuration? The number is meaningless without naming the baseline and ideally giving absolute parameter counts per benchmark (since the reduction will vary with the number of edge types).

**4. The ablation claim is underspecified.**
"Approximately 1.0% on average" — average over what? Three benchmarks? Multiple seeds? Report per-benchmark numbers with variance, or at minimum say "averaged across the three benchmarks (single run each)."

If the only baseline is your own prior model and the gains are within noise, calling this a "successor" invites the reader to ask why it is a separate paper. Either treat v1 as one baseline among several (preferable) or be explicit that this is an incremental architectural study.
