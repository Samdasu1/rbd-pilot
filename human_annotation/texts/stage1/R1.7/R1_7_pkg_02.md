Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) are widely deployed for
solving partial differential equations. A central challenge is
balancing the residual-loss term against boundary-condition and
data-fitting losses, which can lead to training instability. Recent
analysis (Wang et al., 2021) has shown that proper weighting of the
residual loss yields stable training, removing the need for special
optimization tricks.

Building on this stability result, we propose AdaptivePINN, a method
that automatically tunes residual-loss weights using a moving average
of gradient norms. AdaptivePINN does not require any manual
hyperparameter sweeps for the residual weight, in contrast to prior
work which required dataset-specific tuning.

We evaluate AdaptivePINN on three benchmark PDEs: 1D heat equation,
2D Burgers equation, and 2D Navier-Stokes flow. AdaptivePINN
converges in 60% of the iterations required by uniform-weight
baselines and reaches lower terminal residual loss across all three.
The training curves in Figure 3 are smooth, supporting Wang et al.'s
stability findings.

We position AdaptivePINN as a practical extension of the stability
theory, removing the manual-tuning bottleneck while preserving the
theoretical guarantees of the underlying weighting framework. This
framing motivates the rest of the paper. We further provide a small
ablation on the moving-average window length and find that the method
is robust to that choice across a 4x range of values, supporting our
claim that no manual hyperparameter tuning is required.
