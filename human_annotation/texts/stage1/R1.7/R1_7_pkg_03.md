To test whether findings at fleet size N=200 generalize to
operationally relevant fleet sizes, we deploy all strategies at
N up to 10,000 using the same RL checkpoints trained at N=200.
Three patterns emerge from the scaling experiments.

First, the combined strategy maintains 100 percent commitment
and full market eligibility at all fleet sizes. Its components —
LP planner, gradient optimizer, dispatcher, and inter-window
coupling — operate on the aggregate distribution or per-vehicle
state inspection. None depends on the absolute fleet size, so
commitment is structurally fleet-size-invariant.

Second, PPO and CMDP commitment remains at 40 to 50 percent
regardless of fleet size, confirming that the deployment gap is
a structural property of the rolling re-planning mismatch rather
than a fleet-size artifact. The slight decline at large N
originates from downstream environment dynamics: larger fleets
produce more deterministic aggregate trajectories, lowering the
probability that an arbitrage-driven policy accidentally matches
prior commitments.

Third, predictive LP exhibits a sharp degradation from 91 percent
to 40 percent between N=200 and N=1000. The transition model was
trained on fleets of 50 to 400 vehicles; at N ≥ 1000 it
extrapolates beyond its training distribution. The transition
dynamics change qualitatively as the aggregate distribution
smooths.

Learned parameters thus fail along two distinct axes: the
Lagrangian is fragile to horizon shift while the transition
model is fragile to fleet-size shift. Both are instances of
distribution shift but with different mechanisms.
