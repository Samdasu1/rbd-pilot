Summary: I revised the draft for clarity, tightened ordering so the conservative LP and combined method are introduced before the comparative framing, softened several promotional claims, and flagged passages whose technical content I cannot verify from the excerpt alone.

---

**Revised draft**

The conservative LP scheduler is the simplest optimization-based strategy we consider. At each re-planning step, it solves a linear program that minimizes energy cost over the remaining horizon, allocating charging and discharging power as separate decision variables whose difference defines the aggregate signal. The LP enforces aggregate energy balance, per-step discharge caps, and a total discharge budget; the budget uses conservative utilization factors so that planned trajectories remain feasible under realized fleet availability.

The combined strategy augments this conservative LP with three components: (i) a neural transition model that enables gradient-based refinement of the LP schedule, (ii) a reachability-aware dispatcher that enforces vehicle-level quality-of-service constraints, and (iii) a coupling mechanism that preserves commitment continuity across re-planning boundaries. To our knowledge, it is the first model-based scheduler in this setting to incorporate inter-window coupling directly into the LP objective while retaining full arbitrage flexibility, though we have not exhaustively surveyed adjacent literatures.

The pipeline runs in three stages. First, the LP planner emits an initial trajectory subject to fleet-level and inter-window constraints. Second, the gradient-based optimizer refines this trajectory through rollouts of the learned transition model. Third, the dispatcher maps the refined aggregate signal into per-vehicle commands. Under rolling re-planning, each step produces a schedule that the subsequent LP must satisfy within a tight commitment band; by construction, any LP-feasible schedule satisfies this commitment constraint.

We use the combined strategy as the model-based reference point against which RL approaches are evaluated in the remainder of the paper. Whether it is suitable for deployment in commitment-binding markets depends on factors — transition-model accuracy, dispatcher robustness, and commitment-band width — that we examine in the experimental sections.

---

**Notes on the changes**

1. *Ordering.* The original placed the comparative claim ("strongest available model-based reference") before the pipeline description. I moved the method exposition first, then the role-in-evaluation framing, so the reader understands what the method *is* before being told what it is *for*.

2. *Softened claims.*
   - "The first model-based scheduling method that integrates …" became "To our knowledge, … in this setting," with an explicit caveat about survey scope. The original phrasing is a strong priority claim that typically requires a supporting citation sweep.
   - "Strongest available model-based reference" was removed; "available" is ambiguous (available where? to whom?), and the superlative is hard to defend without a benchmark table. The replacement simply names the role.
   - "Deployment-ready scheduling architecture" was the most promotional phrase in the draft and was replaced with a conditional statement pointing to the evidence that would actually license a deployment claim.

3. *Sentence-level prose.*
   - "It augments the conservative LP with three components" was kept but the components were renumbered (i)–(iii) for parsability.
   - "The LP allocates charging power and discharging power separately, with the aggregate signal defined as their difference" was compressed into a single clause.
   - "Combined therefore serves as …" — the "therefore" did not follow from what preceded it; the connective was dropped.
   - "Any LP-feasible schedule automatically satisfies the commitment constraint" — "automatically" reads as hand-wave; "by construction" is more precise if (and only if) the LP encodes the commitment band as a hard constraint. See caveat below.

4. *Logical tightening.* The original asserted both that the combined method preserves "full arbitrage flexibility" and that it enforces a "tight commitment band." These can coexist, but the draft did not explain how. I left the claim intact in the revision but flag it as something the surrounding text should justify; if arbitrage flexibility is reduced inside the band, the wording should reflect that.
