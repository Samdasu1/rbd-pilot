```
Cross-lingual sentiment analysis has become an active subfield of NLP
in recent years. Our work introduces SentiBridge, the first cross-lingual
sentiment classifier that achieves competitive performance across both
high-resource and low-resource languages. We base this claim on the
novel training procedure that combines multilingual contrastive
pretraining with a sentiment-specific fine-tuning head, as introduced
by Smith et al. (2022).

Existing methods either focus on individual high-resource languages
or use translation-based pipelines, which suffer from translation
drift. Our cross-lingual approach instead operates directly on the
target language without translation, an idea also explored by Liu
et al. (2019), who demonstrated improvements on Romance and Germanic
languages.

SentiBridge extends the architecture of Smith et al. with an
attention-based pooling layer and a sentiment polarity head trained
jointly on six languages: English, Spanish, French, German, Mandarin,
and Arabic. Training uses 1.2M sentiment-labeled examples per language
drawn from public corpora.

We evaluate on the XNLI-sentiment subset (5 languages) and the SST
test set (English). On Spanish, our model reaches 81.3% accuracy
compared to translation-pipeline baseline of 78.4%. On Mandarin, we
reach 76.1% versus 72.8% baseline. The detailed per-language results
are in Table 4.

We position SentiBridge as a meaningful contribution to multilingual
sentiment analysis, particularly for languages where direct training
data is scarce.
```

```
## Major issues

**1. The "first" claim is not supported and likely false.**
The opening states SentiBridge is "the first cross-lingual sentiment classifier that achieves competitive performance across both high-resource and low-resource languages." The text gives no survey, no defined criterion for "competitive," and no definition of "low-resource" — and indeed the six training languages listed (English, Spanish, French, German, Mandarin, Arabic) are all high-resource by standard usage. The paper does not actually evaluate on a low-resource language, so the headline claim is unsupported by the evidence presented and should be removed or substantially weakened.

**2. Internal contradiction about novelty.**
The abstract grounds the contribution in "the novel training procedure ... as introduced by Smith et al. (2022)." A procedure introduced by prior work cannot be the novel contribution of this paper. Either (a) Smith et al. introduced the procedure and this paper's contribution lies elsewhere (the attention pooling + joint sentiment head + multilingual joint training), or (b) the procedure is genuinely new here and the Smith et al. attribution is wrong. Pick one and rewrite. As written, the novelty story is incoherent.

The text says existing methods "either focus on individual high-resource languages or use translation-based pipelines," and then immediately says the no-translation cross-lingual idea was "also explored by Liu et al.
```
