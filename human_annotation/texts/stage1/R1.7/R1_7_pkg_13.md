```
Approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) search has become a fundamental
primitive in machine learning, supporting retrieval-augmented
generation, recommendation, and large-scale clustering. Classical
methods include locality-sensitive hashing (LSH; Indyk and Motwani,
1998), product quantization (PQ; Jegou et al., 2011), and tree-based
partitioning such as k-d trees and random projection trees.

Recent work has extended these foundations in three directions:
(1) learned indices that adapt the partition structure to the data
distribution, (2) graph-based methods that traverse a navigable
structure of approximate nearest neighbors, and (3) compression-aware
quantization that trades precision for memory at scale.

Among graph-based approaches, ScaNN (Guo et al., 2020) introduced
anisotropic vector quantization combined with score-aware
partitioning. DiskANN (Subramanya et al., 2019) showed that on-disk
graphs with carefully designed greedy search achieve sub-millisecond
latency at billion-scale. NSG (Fu et al., 2019) provided theoretical
guarantees on graph navigability.

We build on these foundations and propose FastNav, a graph-based ANN
method that combines anisotropic compression with disk-resident
traversal. Our contribution is a hybrid index that achieves the
recall of in-memory graph methods at storage costs of compressed
methods. We evaluate on standard benchmarks (SIFT1B, Deep1B, BIGANN)
and report 1.3x speedup over the strongest prior baseline at the
same recall@10. These results establish FastNav as a practical
option for billion-scale ANN deployment in production retrieval
systems.
```
