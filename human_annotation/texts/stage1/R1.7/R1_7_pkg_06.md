Summary: I reviewed the section for unsupported claims, framing/contribution clarity, evidence-claim mismatches, and prose issues, and flagged several places where the language overreaches what the reported evidence can support.

## Major issues

**1. "First" and "clinically relevant accuracy" are unsupported and likely overclaims.**
The opening sentence makes two strong claims simultaneously: (a) priority ("the first machine learning method"), and (b) a performance threshold ("clinically relevant accuracy"). Neither is substantiated.
- Priority claims of this kind require either a systematic survey of prior work or substantial qualification. There is a sizable existing literature on ML-based binding affinity prediction (DeepDTA, GNINA, DiffDock-family methods, EquiBind, RoseTTAFold-AA, etc.), so "first" is almost certainly false unless heavily qualified.
- "Clinically relevant accuracy" is a normative claim that needs an explicit, cited threshold. MAE values of 0.79–0.91 kcal/mol are reasonable for the field but are not self-evidently "clinically relevant" — clinical relevance depends on use case (hit-finding vs. lead optimization vs. potency prediction), and a ~0.85 kcal/mol error corresponds to a factor of ~4× in Ki/Kd, which is borderline at best for many decision contexts.

Suggested rewrite: "We introduce DrugDock, a graph neural network for drug–target binding affinity prediction that achieves competitive accuracy across three target classes (mean absolute error 0.79–0.91 kcal/mol)."

**2. "Diverse target classes" is overstated.**
Three families (kinases, GPCRs, proteases) is a useful but narrow slice of the druggable proteome. It excludes ion channels, transporters, nuclear receptors, protein–protein interfaces, and most allosteric sites. The phrase "diverse target classes" appears twice and in both cases should be softened (e.g., "three major target families").

**3. Generalization claim in the closing paragraph outruns the evidence.**
"Consistent cross-target performance suggest[s] broad applicability in the pharmaceutical industry" is a leap from three families to industry-wide applicability. It also conflates retrospective held-out test performance with prospective deployment utility, which are very different. Recommend either removing this sentence or rewriting it as a hypothesis to be tested, e.g.: "Whether this consistency extends to additional target families and to prospective screening remains to be evaluated."

**4. Evidence–claim alignment: the 12% and 28% improvements need more context.**
- Against what split? Random vs. scaffold vs. target-stratified? With binding-affinity work this matters enormously, since random splits routinely overstate performance by 2–5×.
- Are the deltas statistically significant? Over how many seeds? With what confidence intervals?
- The "chemistry-only baseline that we constructed for this paper" is a self-built comparator. Comparing against a stronger published baseline (e.g., a recent structure-aware method) would make the 28% number more meaningful; otherwise it risks reading as a strawman.
- No absolute MAE for v1 or the baseline is given, so the relative numbers cannot be cross-checked against the per-class numbers later.

**5. Data provenance and overlap.**
The training data is described as the previous dataset plus 50,000 new measurements from "our group's recent crystallography campaigns." Two concerns:
- Is the held-out test set disjoint from both the prior dataset *and* the new measurements at the level of (a) ligand scaffold and (b) protein sequence/binding site? Without this, the reported MAEs may not reflect generalization.
- A binding-affinity dataset built primarily from one group's crystallography campaigns may carry chemotype and target biases that should be acknowledged.
